## The problem
Most UI writing is built around the job to be done — get the user to the next step. But with
generative features, "the next step" isn't the whole risk. People can *over-trust* AI output,
or lose track of when they're acting on a machine's judgment. The job-to-be-done lens doesn't
cover that.

## The reframe
I wrote for a different need: helping users **orient themselves with AI in the moment** — to
know what they're looking at, how much to trust it, and where their own judgment still has to
come in. That's an emotional and trust need, not a task need, and it changes what the words
have to do.

## The approach
- Mapped the AI surfaces in the experience against Responsible-AI maturity — where each one
  sits, and where over-reliance was most likely.
- Translated that into concrete **overreliance-prevention UX strategies** — patterns for
  calibrating trust: signalling uncertainty, inviting verification, adding friction before
  high-consequence actions rather than smoothing everything.

## Why it matters
This is the clearest evidence of how I see content design: not producing strings, but reading
context and emotion — designing for the human's relationship with the system, especially when
that system speaks through AI. It's also where my work meets RAI and product trust directly.
